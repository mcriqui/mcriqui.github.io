Challenge

Most, if not all methods used in design thinking have been designed around co-location. These methods break down on conference call when attention spans wane and typical design exercises are not as engaging.  Our challenge was to tackle

remote design thinking

Exploring the Problem

With such a big problem space, we had to constrain ourselves. Through affinity diagramming and brainstorming sessions, we constrained ourselves to the

beginning of the design process specifically focusing on idea generation.

User Research

We interviewed several User Experience Designers and a Software Developer who works remotely. We found that there were many issues with idea generation and sketching early on in the design process. Our key insight was that

non-designers are unfamiliar with the design process and are reluctant to sketch.

Solution

We created DeScribe, a tool that revamps evocative sketching into a remote digital co-design tool. Scroll through the slideshow to see DeScribe in action.

This is Phillip Hughes, a UX Designer that is planning the remote codesign session. He decides to use Describe to invite some participants to join the session.

 

Through Describe, Philip enters the number of participants to join in the meeting before it starts.  He checks his calendar invitation and sees that four people have accepted including himself, so he enters 4.

Philip prepares for his meeting by making 4 sketches which will be used to start the session. He sketches on the interactive whiteboard within Describe and saves them.

Philip’s sketches are successfully uploaded. Now he can begin the session allowing the three other participants to join the session.

Shelby is a user. She receives one of the sketches and writes what it is. The software randomly sends one sketch to each of the participants. If she doesn’t know how to answer, she can receive probing questions to help her answer.

 

Colleen is another user. She randomly receives the description Shelby wrote and sketches what that means to her. Again, the software randomly sends one of the descriptions to each of the participants.

At the end of the session, Nicole, a developer, views all the sketches and chooses her favorite ones. All of the participants have the ability to do this.

Nicole also fills in her overall feedback about the sketches. There are questions to prompt Nicole if she doesn’t know what to write but she can give whatever feedback she chooses.

When the session is over, Philip can view all of the sketches, ideas and favorited items. He can use this information to improve the project’s design, or as a starting point for further brainstorming.

How it Works

The meeting organizer creates the original sketches that are randomly send to participants. From there, sketches and text are randomly sent to users.

Testing

We tested out the method by providing people with ideas or sketches and asking them to translate them into the opposite. Then, we took their results and swapped them amongst the participants. This test generated several new ideas and validated our concept.

Why Describe?

Rudimentary Sketching

The method puts designers and non-designers on a level playing field. Each person gets either a sketch or text, and they have complete freedom to show their imagination. Because the tool only allows simple pencil drawings on a computer, it balances out a designer’s finesse with other products.

 

No Sketch after a Sketch

When a person gets a sketch, they don’t have to draw a sketch of their own. We did this because there is an anxiety that comes with a need to maintain fidelity and finesse at the other sketch. In our previous encounters with non-designers, they would simply decide not to sketch because they would be overwhelmed by iterating on well designed sketches.

 

Latency of Audio and Video

We shied away from audio and video in Describe because latency issues as well as additional setup requirements would take participants away from the actual experience of ideation. With the tool as of now, they can focus on the ideation and collaborate faster.

 

 

 

Works Better Remotely

The sketch-text-sketch cycle works better when the participants can’t see what each other is doing. By default, communicating remotely helps with that situation. In fact, it is easier to compartmentalize the individual contribution in that way, while keeping the overall collaboration smooth and efficient.

 

Synchronous Communication

The method has been designed for synchronous communication. Once a participant receives a sketch and submits their text about it, it is expected that they will receive someone else’s text to sketch out quickly. However, other implementations of the method can allow for asynchronous communication as well.

 

Untapped Potential

The non-designer participants, unlike the designers have a fresh perspective on the ideas. They haven’t worked on the particular project or designs for hours. They probably will not suffer from a pigeonhole effect as a team of designers working on the problem for a long time. They can provide their fresh perspective, preconceived notions and world views to help throw a new light on projects.

Extra Time:

Find a way to engage users when they are done with their part and have extra time.

Unclear Sketches: Sketches could be difficult to decipher because the user is a novice to sketching and using a computer based drawing program.

Reflection

This project was difficult because there are many issues with video and audio conferencing that we as designers can not fix. We had to work within the constraints of unreliable technology. As such, our group make the decision to focus on the strengths of technology and capitalize on them. With that said, our design would need more testing to gauge it's true potential in helping designs and associated stakeholders generate ideas in the beginning of the design process. We had fun with this designers and think others would as well.
